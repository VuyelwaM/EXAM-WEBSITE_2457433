Response To Ethnocomputing

Ethnomathematics confronts the assumption that certain cultural practices are unrelated to mathematics. By investigating mathematical concepts within these practices, ethnomathematics seeks to unveil the hidden mathematical richness. However, accurately capturing the mathematical essence of cultural artifacts or practices can be complex. Creating models often raises questions regarding the authenticity of the mathematical representation and its alignment with indigenous cognitive frameworks. The concept of ethnocomputing proposes the integration of computational media to tackle the challenges faced by ethnomathematics. Computational models offer benefits beyond simulating patterns. They provide a means to explore the epistemological status of mathematical knowledge embedded in cultural practices. Computational tools allow for the examination of intricate relationships, revealing insights into the artisans' perspectives and problem-solving strategies. The essay presents a case study of Navajo weaving to illustrate the application of ethnocomputing. The weaving patterns exhibited a recurring angle of approximately 30 degrees, prompting inquiry into its significance. Through conversations with a weaver, the connection between the angle and the weaving technique "up one over one" emerged. Exploring further, it became apparent that alternative angles resulted in jagged edges, raising the question of whether the weavers employed anti-aliasing techniques similar to those used in computer graphics. Ethnocomputing offers two significant advantages. Firstly, certain pattern generation systems are better understood through the lens of computer science, emphasizing the interdisciplinary nature of mathematical investigations. Secondly, the conceptual framework of computing enables a deeper understanding of artisans' perspectives when analogous computational processes exist. Ethnocomputing opens avenues for exploring iterative algorithms, shaping complex shapes, and uncovering new mathematical insights. Ethnomathematics has significant implications for education, particularly for underrepresented ethnic groups. Studies indicate that low performance and disinterest in mathematics among African American, Native American, Latino, and Pacific Islander children may be influenced by factors such as oppositional identity formation and the myth of genetic determinism. Ethnomathematics, including ethnocomputing, can challenge these stereotypes and foster cultural connections to mathematics. However, simply presenting photographs of cultural artifacts is insufficient for engaging children and developing their mathematical skills. Computing can contribute to overcoming educational challenges associated with ethnomathematics. The essay highlights a collaboration with Rough Rock, a school run by the Navajo Nation, where a simulation for Navajo weaving was developed. The use of computational media sparked enthusiasm among the weavers, who recognized the potential to maintain the relevance of traditional weaving for future generations.
